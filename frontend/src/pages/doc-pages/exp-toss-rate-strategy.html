Interest Rate Reduction

Background

The Joint Account product initially offered a 1.8% interest rate — identical to regular savings accounts. However, the product’s unique value came from its social and shared-finance features, not from its interest rate. I hypothesized that lowering the rate would not significantly affect user retention or growth, and could instead save substantial costs for the bank.

Problem

The main challenge was balancing cost efficiency with user retention. Reducing interest rates risked user churn if customers perceived the product as less rewarding. To move forward, I needed to gather both quantitative validation and stakeholder alignment across legal, finance, and product divisions.

Solution

  1. Behavioral Data Analysis:
    • Analyzed asset movement during a previous rate reduction (from 2.0% → 1.8%) and compared it with personal accounts.
    • Found that Joint Account users were less sensitive to rate changes due to the product’s social engagement features.
  2. User Research:
    • Conducted 30+ user interviews, confirming that collaboration and shared financial management were the main motivations, not interest rates.
  3. Stakeholder Alignment:
    • Presented findings to over 40 cross-functional stakeholders including executives, compliance, and finance teams.
    • Built consensus around a 1.7% rate reduction, supported by cohort retention analysis and customer insights.
  4. Segmentation Strategy:
    • For interest-sensitive users, created migration paths to alternative savings products to mitigate dissatisfaction.

Result & Analytics

The interest rate adjustment delivered $4.3M in annual savings while maintaining user retention and growth stability. The initiative became a model for data-driven pricing decisions at Toss Bank, showing how strategic experimentation could optimize business efficiency without compromising user trust.
